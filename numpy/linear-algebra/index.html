Matrix multiplication#

The inner dimensions of matrices must match for multiplication. The dimension or output matrix is the outer dimensions, e.g., a 2×32 \times 32×3 multiplied with a 3×33 \times 33×3 matrix will result in a 2×32 \times 32×3 matrix.

Matrix multiplication is not commutative (in general): AB≠BAAB \neq BAAB=BA

System of linear equations#

The central problem of linear algebra is solving the system of linear equations. There are two main methods to solve linear equations: (1) method of elimination and (2) Cramer's rule: method of determinants.

Let us consider the case of nnn linear equations with nnn unknowns. In case of elimination, the multiples of first equation is subtracted from the remaining equations to eliminate the first unknown. This leaves a smaller system of (n−1)(n-1)(n−1) equations with (n−1)(n-1)(n−1) unknowns. The method is repeated until we are left with one equation with one unknown. Then we substitute the solution and find the other unknowns in reversed ordered. This method is generally used in practice to solve system of linear equation, and known as Gaussian elimination.

2x1+x2+x3=12 x_1 + x_2 + x_3 = 12x1​+x2​+x3​=1
6x1+2x2+x3=−16 x_1 + 2 x_2 + x_3 = -16x1​+2x2​+x3​=−1
−2x1+2x2+x3=7-2 x_1 + 2 x_2 + x_3 = 7−2x1​+2x2​+x3​=7

Matrix equation: Ax=bA\textbf{x} = \textbf{b}Ax=b

Solution: x=A−1b\textbf{x} = A^{-1} \textbf{b}x=A−1b

[211621−221][x1x2x3]=[1−17]\begin{bmatrix}2 & 1 & 1 \\ 6 & 2 & 1 \\ -2 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 7 \end{bmatrix}⎣⎡​26−2​122​111​⎦⎤​⎣⎡​x1​x2​x3​​⎦⎤​=⎣⎡​1−17​⎦⎤​

LU factorization#

After the Gauss elimination, we are left with an upper triangular matrix (UUU).

Ux=cU\textbf{x} = \textbf{c}Ux=c

The matrix operation that relates AAA and UUU (b\textbf{b}b and c\textbf{c}c) is found to be a lower triangular matrix LLL.

A=LUA = LUA=LU
Ax=LUx=Lc=bA\textbf{x} = LU\textbf{x} = L\textbf{c} = \textbf{b}Ax=LUx=Lc=b

  • Output
import scipy.linalg as lap, l, u = la.lu(A)print("L=", l)print("U=", u)
L= [[ 1.          0.          0.        ] [-0.33333333  1.          0.        ] [ 0.33333333  0.125       1.        ]]U= [[6.         2.         1.        ] [0.         2.66666667 1.33333333] [0.         0.         0.5       ]]
  • Input
  • Output
np.matmul(l, u)
array([[ 6.,  2.,  1.],       [-2.,  2.,  1.],       [ 2.,  1.,  1.]])

Notice that the order of the rows changed and this information is contained in the permutaion matrix PPP.

The diagonal elements of LLL are always 1. There is another form of decomposition A=LDUA = LDUA=LDU, where both LLL and UUU has diagonal elements as 1, and DDD is the diagonal matrix of pivots. For a nonsingular matrix AAA, the LULULU and LDULDULDU factorization are unique.

Rank of a matrix is the number of pivots or the number of non-zero rows in UUU. It represents the number of independent rows in the matrix. Note that the same number of columns in AAA are linearly independent as well.

Projections#

The projection of a vector b\textbf{b}b onto a\textbf{a}a is:

p=aTbaTaap = \frac{a^T b}{a^T a} ap=aTaaTb​a

The projection matrix P=aaTaTaP = \frac{a a^T}{a^T a}P=aTaaaT​ so that p=Pbp = Pbp=Pb.

PPP is a symmetric matrix, and P2=PP^2 = PP2=P
